H200, B200, B300, GB200 & GB300

A deployment-oriented comparison of eight-GPU HGX/DGX-class nodes and Grace Blackwell NVL72 racks, followed by what official SGLang articles actually change in the serving configuration.

H200 + B200 observed 10 July 2026 B300 + GB series published references SGLang review updated 13 July 2026 No benchmark or workload run
GB200 and GB300 are not ordinary eight-GPU nodes. Their main deployment product is an NVL72 rack: 18 independently booted compute trays, each with four GPUs and two Grace CPUs, joined into one 72-GPU NVLink scale-up domain. The charts therefore compare each platform's native scale-up unit, while the tables expose tray and per-GPU context where needed.

Takeaway

The GB prefix changes the system boundary.

H200/B200/B300 stop at an eight-GPU NVSwitch node. GB200/GB300 couple GPUs to Grace CPUs and extend the scale-up domain across all 18 trays in an NVL72 rack.

GB300 adds capacity, not more rack NVLink.

Versus GB200, NVIDIA publishes 20 versus 13.4 TB GPU memory and 1,080 versus 720 dense FP4 PFLOPS, while both retain 130 TB/s rack NVLink and 576 TB/s HBM bandwidth.

SGLang changes the parallelism recipe.

Its GB articles use PD disaggregation, DP attention, wide expert parallelism, low precision, Grace-memory offload, pipeline-parallel prefill, MTP, and topology-aware orchestration.

These bars compare native scale-up units: one eight-GPU node for H200/B200/B300 and one 72-GPU rack for GB200/GB300. They are deployment totals, not per-GPU speedups.

Official SGLang Deployment Evidence

Yes, the official SGLang articles discuss deployment configuration in concrete terms. They are DeepSeek V3/R1 performance recipes tied to specific commits, precisions, and workloads. Treat the shapes below as architectural evidence, not as universal current defaults or commands to paste unchanged.

GB200 NVL72 · PD + large-scale EP

Part I used 14 of the rack's 18 compute trays: two trays / eight GPUs for prefill and twelve trays / 48 GPUs for decode. The reproduction flags pair TP=8, DP=8 on prefill with TP=48, DP=48 on decode and enable DP attention, DeepEP, and Mooncake KV transfer.

2 trays · 8 GPUs

Decode pool

12 trays · 48 GPUs

In-rack fabric

NVLink-aware DeepEP and Mooncake paths; scale-out RDMA avoided within the rack.

Part II scales low-precision prefill down to two ranks, versus four for the higher-precision path, while retaining 48 decode ranks. Grace CPU memory is used for prefetched weight offload when reducing EP size.

GB300 NVL72 · long-context PD

The 128K/8K recipe is orchestrated with NVIDIA Dynamo. Prefill uses chunked pipeline parallelism and dynamic chunking; decode uses Wide-EP with DP attention, FP8 KV cache, NVFP4 MoE, and MTP through SGLang's overlap scheduler.

Prefill path

PP prefill; PP4 kernel study; 32K dynamic chunking produced the best reported TTFT.

Decode path

Wide-EP / DP attention up to 32 ranks; larger HBM supports higher active concurrency.

Control plane

Dynamo + srt-slurm in reproduction; Dynamo Kubernetes supports topology-aware scheduling and autoscaling.

The fixed reproduction used SGLang v0.5.7-era code, CUDA 13.0, rebuilt sgl-kernel, and FlashInfer 0.6.1 or newer for SM103 CuTe DSL support. Those are reproduction facts, not current minimum-version guidance.

Serving Implications

Think tray, rack, then cluster

A GB worker crosses four-GPU tray boundaries while remaining inside NVLink. Resource labels and affinity rules need a rack-domain identity, not only host and GPU IDs.

Wide EP becomes practical

The 72-GPU domain lowers the communication penalty for distributing MoE experts broadly, reducing per-GPU weight traffic and enabling DP attention alongside expert parallelism.

GB300 primarily expands long-context headroom

SGLang attributes much of its matched-latency gain to larger HBM, higher active decode batches, fewer retractions, and faster softmax-heavy attention rather than higher rack HBM bandwidth.

Grace memory is not ordinary host DRAM

The GB200 recipe deliberately uses the high-bandwidth Grace coupling for prefetched offload. Whether that wins depends on model layout, EP size, kernels, and synchronization saved.

PD orchestration is a first-class dependency

At rack scale, routing, KV transfer, health state, and heterogeneous worker lifecycles affect delivered throughput. SGLang's GB300 work uses Dynamo rather than treating P and D as one monolithic server.

Facility and failure domains grow

A 120–142 kW liquid-cooled rack is a materially larger scheduling and operational unit than one 8-GPU server. A tray restart is local; a rack fabric or cooling event can affect the full 72-GPU domain.

Performance numbers are not carried into the hardware bars. The SGLang articles use specific DeepSeek checkpoints, precisions, sequence lengths, latency targets, and historical software revisions. They prove that GB topology changes useful deployment choices; they do not provide a universal GB200/GB300 multiplier for GLM-5.2 or another model.

Method and Sources

Observed evidence

  • Only the Europe H200 and Northwest B200 machines were passively inspected.
  • nvidia-smi, topology, CPU, memory, PCIe, RDMA, and storage inventory were read without starting a workload.
  • The observations remain point-in-time samples, not claims about every H200 or B200 system.

Published evidence

  • B300, GB200, and GB300 values come from current NVIDIA product pages and system/reference-architecture guides.
  • GB deployment consequences come from official SGLang Team articles and their linked reproduction issues.
  • No B300, GB200, or GB300 cluster access path was used or guessed.

Explicitly not run

  • No CUDA context, PyTorch import, model request, synthetic kernel, or GPU benchmark.
  • No CPU, disk, network, collective, or RDMA traffic test.
  • No package installation, compilation, serving changes, or Kubernetes mutations.
